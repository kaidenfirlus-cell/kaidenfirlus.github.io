A binomial is a polynomial with exactly two terms (e.g., 6x − 8, x² − 9). Factoring a binomial means expressing it as a product of simpler polynomials. Common binomial factoring patterns:

  • Common factor: e.g., 6x − 8 = 2(3x − 4).
  • Difference of squares: a² − b² = (a − b)(a + b). Example: x² − 9 = (x − 3)(x + 3).
  • Over the reals: some binomials that are not difference-of-squares may still factor into linear factors with irrational coefficients (e.g., 6x² − 8 = (√6 x − 2√2)(√6 x + 2√2)).

A trinomial is a three-term polynomial (e.g., x² + 5x + 6 or 2x² + 7x + 3). Factoring a trinomial means writing it as the product of two binomials (when possible). Typical cases:

  • Monic trinomial (a = 1): x² + bx + c -> find integers r, s with r + s = b and r·s = c, then x² + bx + c = (x + r)(x + s). Example: x² + 5x + 6 = (x + 2)(x + 3).
  • Non-monic trinomial (a ≠ 1): ax² + bx + c -> common techniques are the "ac-method" (split middle term), factoring by grouping, or trying integer factor pairs of a and c to find integer binomial factors.
  • Special trinomial forms: perfect square trinomials (a² ± 2ab + b² = (a ± b)²) and trinomials that factor by grouping.

Skill Development — Factoring & Quadratic Manipulation

1) Expanding and simplifying a quadratic expression

To expand a quadratic expression, distribute and combine like terms. Example: expand (2x + 3)(x − 4). First distribute: (2x)(x) + (2x)(−4) + (3)(x) + (3)(−4) = 2x² − 8x + 3x − 12. Combine like terms: 2x² − 5x − 12. Always collect like terms and simplify coefficients to finish.

Worked example: Expand and simplify (x + 2)(x + 5): x·x + x·5 + 2·x + 2·5 = x² + 5x + 2x + 10 = x² + 7x + 10.

2) Common factoring (factoring out the greatest common factor)

Look for the greatest common factor (GCF) of all terms and factor it out. This reduces coefficients and sometimes reveals simpler factoring patterns.

Worked example: Factor 4x² + 6x = 2(2x² + 3x) = 2x(2x + 3) if x is common to all terms. Always factor out the largest quantity common to every term.

3) Factoring quadratics in the form x² + bx + c (monic, a = 1)

For x² + bx + c, find two numbers r and s such that r + s = b and r·s = c. Then x² + bx + c = (x + r)(x + s). This is the fastest method when a = 1. If integer pairs do not exist, the trinomial may not factor over the integers.

Worked example: Factor x² + 7x + 10. Find r, s with r+s=7 and r·s=10 → r=2, s=5. So x² + 7x + 10 = (x + 2)(x + 5).

4) Factoring quadratics in the form ax² + bx + c where a is not 1 (a has no common factor)

Multiple reliable methods exist:

  • ac-method (split the middle term): Multiply a·c, find two numbers that multiply to a·c and add to b, split bx into those two terms, and factor by grouping.
  • Trial of factor pairs: Try integer factor pairs of a and c to guess binomial factors (useful when a and c are small).
  • Grouping: After splitting the middle term, factor common factors from each pair to extract binomial factors.

Worked example: Factor 6x² + 11x + 3. Compute a·c = 18. Find two numbers that multiply to 18 and add to 11 → 9 and 2. Split 11x → 9x + 2x: 6x² + 9x + 2x + 3. Group: (6x² + 9x) + (2x + 3) = 3x(2x + 3) + 1(2x + 3) = (2x + 3)(3x + 1).

5) Factoring cases (common patterns and how to recognize them)

Recognize the most common factoring cases quickly:

  • Greatest common factor (always check first): pull out GCF.
  • Difference of squares: a² − b² = (a − b)(a + b).
  • Perfect square trinomial: a² ± 2ab + b² = (a ± b)².
  • Factor by grouping: useful when four-term polynomials or after you split the middle term in the ac-method.
  • Non-integer/irrational factors: if integer factoring fails, real linear factors may still exist and involve radicals.

Always state the domain you are allowing for coefficients (integers, rationals, reals). Over the integers or rationals factoring may fail even though real factoring (using radicals) exists.

Application — The y-intercept in quadratic applications

In quadratic applications the y-intercept (the value of y when x = 0) is often the most direct, concrete starting point of a model: it represents the initial state or baseline quantity before any change occurs. For example, if a quadratic models profit over different production levels, the y-intercept gives the profit when production is zero (commonly a fixed cost or seed revenue). If a parabola models the height of a projectile over time, the y-intercept is the height at launch time; if a quadratic models the concentration of a chemical after some manufacturing step, the y-intercept can indicate the starting concentration. Because the y-intercept is obtained simply by evaluating the function at 0, it is easy to check experimentally or measure directly, which makes it especially valuable for sanity checks: if field measurements disagree with the y-intercept the model needs revising. In policy, economics, engineering or physics contexts, the y-intercept often carries physical or economic meaning (initial cost, initial height, starting population, baseline dose), so it constrains what models are reasonable and ensures predictions stay anchored to reality.

Quadratic Problem — Rectangle with Area 6x² − 8

A rectangle has area A(x) = 6x² − 8. To find its dimensions, we factor the expression. The greatest common factor of the terms is 2, giving 6x² − 8 = 2(3x² − 4). This immediately provides one valid pair of dimensions based on integer-polynomial factoring.

From this factorization, the rectangle can have dimensions 2 and 3x² − 4. These represent the simplest polynomial lengths that multiply to the given area. Although other algebraic combinations exist, such as 1 × (6x² − 8), they do not offer a more meaningful geometric interpretation of the rectangle’s dimensions.

We can confirm that no integer or rational linear factors exist. Any attempt to express the quadratic as (ax + b)(cx + d) fails, because matching coefficients would require specific integer pairs that simultaneously satisfy ac = 6 and bd = −8 while eliminating the middle term. No such combination exists.

Over the real numbers, however, the quadratic does factor into linear expressions with irrational constants. A correct real factorization is (√6x − 2√2)(√6x + 2√2). This alternative confirms that multiple dimension pairs are possible, but the only standard integer-polynomial factorization remains 2 and 3x² − 4.
