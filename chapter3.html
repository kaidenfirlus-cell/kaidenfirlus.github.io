Skill Development — Chapter 3

1) Solving a Quadratic Equation by Graphing

To solve a quadratic by graphing, plot its function and locate where it crosses the x-axis. These x-intercepts correspond to the equation’s zeros or roots. If the parabola doesn’t cross the axis, it has no real solutions.

2) Solving a Quadratic Equation by Factoring

Rewrite the quadratic in factored form. Then, apply the zero product property: if (x − r₁)(x − r₂) = 0, then x = r₁ or x = r₂. This is an efficient algebraic method when factoring is possible with integer or rational roots.

Application — Chapter 3

In quadratic applications, the zeros are crucial because they often represent meaningful real-world events, such as when an object lands, a projectile returns to the ground, or profit equals zero. Understanding zeros allows us to interpret when and where key moments happen, linking mathematics to real-life behavior and predictions.

Quadratic Problem — Rock Thrown from a Cliff

A rock is thrown from the top of a cliff on an uncharted planet. Its height above the surface is modeled by h(t) = -8t² + 10t + 25, where t is time (seconds) and h(t) is height (metres).

A) When will the rock reach its maximum height?

Since a = -8 (negative), the parabola opens downward, meaning it has a maximum point at its vertex. Using the formula t = -b / (2a):

t = -10 / (2 × -8) = 10 / 16 = 0.625 seconds.

B) What is the maximum height?

Substitute t = 0.625 into h(t):

h(0.625) = -8(0.625)² + 10(0.625) + 25 = -8(0.3906) + 6.25 + 25 = -3.125 + 31.25 = 28.125 metres.

Therefore, the rock reaches its maximum height of 28.125 metres at t = 0.625 seconds after being thrown.
