Completing the square is a method used to rewrite a quadratic equation in vertex form, y = a(x − h)² + k. It helps find the vertex and makes solving quadratics simpler by forming a perfect square trinomial.

The discriminant is the expression b² − 4ac from the quadratic formula. It determines how many and what type of solutions (roots) a quadratic equation has: two, one, or none (real).

The quadratic formula, x = (-b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / (2a), is used to find the roots of any quadratic equation. It always works, even when factoring is not possible.

The vertex form of a quadratic is y = a(x − h)² + k, where (h, k) is the vertex of the parabola. It easily shows the direction of opening and maximum or minimum point.

Skill Development — Chapter 4

1) Completing the Square

To complete the square, divide all terms by the coefficient of x² if needed. Then, take half the coefficient of x, square it, and add it inside the brackets (and balance it outside if needed). Example: x² + 6x + 5 → (x + 3)² − 4.

2) Using the Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula solves ax² + bx + c = 0 by substituting the coefficients into x = (-b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / (2a). This works even when factoring is impossible or when roots are irrational or complex.

3) The Nature of Roots

The discriminant (b² − 4ac) reveals the nature of the roots:

  • If b² − 4ac > 0, two distinct real roots.
  • If b² − 4ac = 0, one real repeated root.
  • If b² − 4ac < 0, two complex (non-real) roots.

Application — Chapter 4

In quadratic applications, the vertex is important because it often represents a maximum or minimum point, key for real-world interpretation. For example, the vertex might represent the highest point of a ball’s flight, the lowest production cost, or the maximum profit achievable. Understanding the vertex helps in optimization problems, allowing predictions and decisions that lead to the best outcomes.

Quadratic Problem — Theatre Company Profits

A theatre company’s profit is modeled by P(x) = -60x² + 1800x + 16500, where x is the ticket price in dollars.

A) What ticket price gives the maximum profit?

The parabola opens downward because a = -60. The vertex gives the maximum point: x = -b / (2a).

x = -1800 / (2 × -60) = -1800 / -120 = 15

Therefore, the company should charge $15 per ticket for maximum profit.

B) What is the maximum profit?

Substitute x = 15 into P(x):

P(15) = -60(15)² + 1800(15) + 16500
= -60(225) + 27000 + 16500
= -13500 + 43500 = $30,000

The maximum profit is $30,000 when ticket prices are set at $15 each.
